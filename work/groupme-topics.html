Role
UX/UI Designer

Problem

GroupMe allows users communicate all kinds of information with everyone in the group. But users have difficulties sharing and finding important or specific messages because:

‚òù ¬†It's hard to find information in a huge pile messages

‚úå There's no way of differentiating certain messages

Solution

The Topics feature allows you to create 'topics' that categorize messages accordingly.

Emphasize important messages with topics

Create topics for your messages. You can access topics that has already been made, or create your own.

Reach a greater audience

Share important information with everyone in the group with the Topics feature. You can see who has seen the message.

Find information you need easily

Find all the topics and relevant messages in one place. Here, you can see the most recent topics mentioned and the most recent message related to the topic.

Process

Initial Assumption

Going into this, my original assumption was that if GroupMe had features like pinning, users could share and find ¬†meaningful messages more easily.

...but what's the actual problem?

From interviewing 5 GroupMe users to find out how people use and feel about existing features on GroupMe, I found that:

‚òù People want to communicate efficiently

"I use GroupMe to tell people what's going on, or to plan or make decisions together"¬†

‚úå People want to reach a greater audience

"I send messages on GroupMe because I want everyone in the group to read it. Otherwise I would use direct messages"¬†

Slack is great, but it's too much.

As part of my market research, I looked at Slack to see how they foster group communication and interactions online. I found that Slack has great features that centers around group environments, like workspaces, channels, threads, pinning, starring, etc.

... but it has a steep learning curve and it's too complex for the casual and instantaneous conversations that happen on GroupMe.

Finding places to improve

With my friends, Allison and Jenny, as my brainstorming buddies, we found 2 solution spaces.

Emphasis
How might we emphasize certain messages from others, so they can be differentiated easily?
Organization
How might we organize the conversations better, so that we can find and share information easily?

What if users could organize their messages?

I sketched around 30 initial design concepts within the solution space and landed on two that I felt were the strongest contenders.

Option A: Announcements
Users can post announcements, separate from group messages ‚Äî like a notice board or Facebook newsfeed.

Option B: Topics
Organize messages on the fly. It's like Slack‚Äôs 'channel' feature, but even more instantaneous.

Option B, the winner

After speaking to both GroupMe users and non-users, the topics feature came out to be more favorable over the other because:

‚òù It doesn't distract the workflow of the primary user flow, like sending a message

‚úå The announcements feature would take away one of GroupMe‚Äôs major qualities ‚Äì instantaneous communication.

üëå it doesn't require extra steps for people to access the information ‚Äì which is important, because people use GroupMe to reach a greater audience

More organization

This is really really important!

The goal of the Topic feature is to organize messages and make them more meaningful to others.To make sure that this goal is achieved, I asked myself some questions:

‚òù How can I let people know that a message is important?

‚úå How can I make sure that a message has reached the audience that it is intended for?

Keeping people accountable

After brainstorming, I got the idea that accountability could place more meaning to messages.

If people know that the sender can see that they have seen it, they could be more incentivized to act upon it. On the flip side, if people know the scope of the message they sent, they would have a better understanding of what kind of messages or content group members find more meaningful.

So, I adapted GroupMe's existing design that shows who liked the message.

To successfully adapt the existing design to the new one, I had to consider two scenarios:

‚òù Seen: when people see the message, but don't like the message

‚úå Seen + Liked: when people like the message (they would've already seen the message)

In the real world, I would limit this function only to messages with topics because otherwise, it would take away the informal aspect of GroupMe. Plus, nobody likes being left on read.

Creating a seamless experience

Creating a topic implies that it will likely be a reoccurring subject in conversations. For example, #social-events could be a reoccurring topic for a campus organization.

To account for this scenario, I explored some designs that would allow users to easily access topics that has been already made.

I decided to go with exploration A because it was more scalable compared to B. Once more and more topics get created, exploration A would be able to display more topics on the screen without the user having to extensively scroll through them.

Conclusion

Group platforms like GroupMe play a huge role in our daily lives, especially today. And GroupMe is only one of many other group platforms like Facebook, Messenger, Slack, and WhatsApp. However, group chats can get inefficient at times. I myself am a part of countless group chats, more than half of which I don‚Äôt check consistently. Most of the time, this is because of the overflowing messages, which makes it difficult for me catch up. With a feature like the Tag feature, users have the ability to organize messages in a way that is more useful. But more importantly, it makes the messages more meaningful, which then creates more meaningful relationships.
